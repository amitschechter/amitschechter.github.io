Current Research Interests

Some of my current research interests include:

Visual Grounding in Vision-Language Models
Despite impressive multimodal capabilities, VLMs often over-rely on language priors and effectively ignore visual input. I am interested in understanding when and why this happens, and in developing methods to improve visual grounding.
Attention and Concept Manipulation
Building on my work in attention redirection for concept erasure, I am interested in the connection between attention redirection and the phenomenon of attention sinks — and whether similar mechanisms can be leveraged for targeted model manipulation beyond erasure.
Structured Visual Generation from Video Models
I am exploring whether structured representations can be extracted or distilled from video generation models, which encode rich spatial and temporal structure. .

Selected Publications & Manuscripts

Concept Erasure via Attention Redirection CVPR Findings 2026
Amit Schechter, Rinon Gal, Ofir Kedem, Gal Chechik, Daniel Cohen-Or
CVPR Findings, 2026
Large text-to-image diffusion models can reproduce copyrighted or sensitive visual concepts, and prior erasure methods suffer from concept re-emergence under adversarial prompting, inpainting, or minor model edits. We introduce ARCE, which trains a per-concept eraser key that competes for attention probability mass in the cross-attention layers, surgically redirecting image patch attention away from target tokens toward a neutral anchor value — leaving the base model frozen and unrelated concepts intact.
[paper]
Weighted Group DRO: Enhancing Subgroup Robustness with Soft Group Assignment Master's Thesis
Amit Schechter, Research Supervisor: Tommi S. Jaakkola
Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 2024
Traditional Group Distributionally Robust Optimization (Group DRO) assumes perfect knowledge of group assignments, yet real-world data often contains noisy or missing group labels. We introduce Weighted Group DRO, a formulation that utilizes soft, probabilistic group assignments to maintain robustness under uncertainty. By leveraging an Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach to estimate latent group probabilities, our method significantly outperforms standard DRO in the presence of label noise and limited annotations on datasets like CelebA and Waterbirds.
[thesis]
CheXaid: Deep Learning Assistance for Physician Diagnosis of Tuberculosis Using Chest X-rays in Patients with HIV npj Digital Medicine
Pranav Rajpurkar*, Chloe O'Connell*, Amit Schechter*, Nishit Asnani, Jason Li, Amirhossein Kiani, Robyn L. Ball, Marc Mendelson, Gary Maartens, Daniël J. van Hoving, Rulan Griesel, Andrew Y. Ng, Tom H. Boyles, Matthew P. Lungren
npj Digital Medicine  ·  Nature Partner Journals, 2021
* Equal contribution
We developed a deep learning algorithm to diagnose tuberculosis in HIV-positive patients from chest X-rays and clinical data, deployed as a web-based diagnostic assistant. In a clinical study across two South African hospitals, AI assistance significantly improved clinician accuracy, while the standalone algorithm achieved 79% accuracy — highlighting its potential in high-burden settings with limited radiological expertise.

Robust Decision Trees via Invariant Risk Minimization Master's Thesis
Amit Schechter, Research Supervisor: Tommi S. Jaakkola
Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 2024
While invariant learning has seen success in neural networks, tree-based models often struggle with out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization. We propose a novel Invariant Decision Tree objective that enforces stage-wise invariance at every split, mitigating the tendency of overparameterized models to degenerate into standard empirical risk minimization. We introduce a "Regret Minimization Tree" variant that balances training accuracy with cross-environment consistency, demonstrating improved generalization on synthetic structural equation models and clinical heart failure datasets.

Other Research & Projects

Don't Judge a Movie by its Poster
CS231n: Deep Learning for Computer Vision  ·  Stanford University, 2018
This project investigates whether deep learning models can overcome human bias to accurately predict a movie's genre using only its promotional poster. We implemented a custom CNN architecture and a ResNet-18 transfer learning model to perform binary and multi-label classification on the TMDB 5000 dataset.
Converting Handwritten Mathematical Expressions into LaTeX
CS229: Machine Learning  ·  Stanford University, 2017
We built an automated system to digitize complex handwritten mathematical equations into LaTeX code to streamline academic document preparation. The system utilizes an SVM for stroke segmentation, compares Neural Networks, CNNs, and Random Forests for character recognition, and employs heuristics for structural analysis. Our approach achieved 86% test accuracy for character classification and demonstrated significant improvements in recognizing rare mathematical symbols through elastic transformation data augmentation.
